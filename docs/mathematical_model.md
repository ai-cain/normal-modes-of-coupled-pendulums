# Mathematical Model of Coupled Pendulums

This document formulates the physical equations for a system of $n$ coupled pendulums, based on the analytical derivation presented in [sbweb Física 3](http://www.sc.ehu.es/sbweb/fisica3/oscilaciones/pendulo_doble/n_pendulos.html).

## 1. System Definition

Consider an idealized system of $n$ consecutive pendulums hanging one from another. To simplify the generalization, we assume all point masses are equal ($m_i = m$) and all segments have the same length ($l_i = L/n$), where $L$ is the total length of the structure.

Let $\theta_i$ be the angular displacement of the $i$-th pendulum with respect to the vertical axis.
For small oscillations ($\sin\theta \approx \theta$), we can linearize the Lagrange equations to construct a matrix system of the form:

$$ M \frac{d^2 \mathbf{\theta}}{dt^2} + K \mathbf{\theta} = \mathbf{0} $$

where $\mathbf{\theta} = \begin{pmatrix} \theta_1 \\ \theta_2 \\ \dots \\ \theta_n \end{pmatrix}$ is the state vector of generalized coordinates.

## 2. Mass Matrix ($M$)

The generalized mass matrix $M$ couples the inertial terms. For $n$ identical masses and segment lengths, its structure is symmetric and defined purely by the remaining pendulums hanging below each node:

$$
M = \begin{pmatrix}
n & n-1 & n-2 & \dots & 1 \\
n-1 & n-1 & n-2 & \dots & 1 \\
n-2 & n-2 & n-2 & \dots & 1 \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
1 & 1 & 1 & \dots & 1
\end{pmatrix}
$$

Programmatically, the elements $M_{i,j}$ (for 1-indexed coordinates) can be generated by:
$$ M_{i,j} = n - \max(i, j) + 1 $$

## 3. Stiffness Matrix ($K$)

The generalized stiffness matrix $K$ arises from the gravitational potential energy. It is a diagonal matrix because gravity acts independently on the vertical angle of each segment:

$$
K = \frac{g}{L/n} \begin{pmatrix}
n & 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\
0 & n-1 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\
0 & 0 & n-2 & \dots & 0 \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 1
\end{pmatrix}
$$

Programmatically, the diagonal elements are:
$$ K_{i,i} = \frac{g}{L/n} (n - i + 1) $$

## 4. Normal Modes and Eigenvalue Problem

The natural frequencies of oscillation (referred to as $\omega$) and their corresponding modal shapes are defined by the generalized eigenvalue problem. Assuming harmonic solutions of the form $\mathbf{\theta}(t) = \mathbf{A} e^{i\omega t}$, the differential equation becomes:

$$ (-\omega^2 M + K) \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{0} $$

Rearranging, we get a standard eigenvalue formulation:

$$ (M^{-1}K) \mathbf{A} = \omega^2 \mathbf{A} $$

### The Spectrum
Solving $\det(M^{-1}K - \omega^2 I) = 0$ yields $n$ eigenvalues, which correspond to the squared natural frequencies $\omega_1^2, \omega_2^2, \dots, \omega_n^2$.

### Modal Vectors
For each eigenvalue $\omega_k^2$, we solve for the corresponding normalized eigenvector $\mathbf{A}_k$.
The components of $\mathbf{A}_k$ dictate the relative amplitude and phase (in-phase or out-of-phase) of each pendulum mass during the $k$-th normal mode of vibration.

## 5. Simulating the Evolution
Once the eigenvalues $\omega_k$ and eigenvectors $\mathbf{A}_k$ are found, any initial condition $\mathbf{\theta}(0), \dot{\mathbf{\theta}}(0)$ can be expressed as a linear superposition of normal modes:

$$ \mathbf{\theta}(t) = \sum_{k=1}^n c_k \mathbf{A}_k \cos(\omega_k t + \phi_k) $$

where $c_k$ and $\phi_k$ are determined by the initial conditions of the system.
